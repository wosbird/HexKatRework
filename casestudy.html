disABILITYsa Responsive Redesign

Project Overview

disABILITYsa is a non-profit organization created to serve San Antonio by making it a more accessible and inclusive city. However, the website lacks accessibility and functionality. We found that users struggled to find pertinent information whether looking for resources or finding information on volunteering. By restructuring the site with simplified and meaningful categorization and navigation and applying universal design principles, we will be able to make disABILITYsa.org as accessible, inclusive, and functional site for all potential users. .

The Challenge

Problems

  • Outdated site - non-responsive design
  • Text-heavy - difficult to scan and problematic for screenreaders
  • Lacks graphical elements - no icons, few images
  • Superfluous menu content - labels are vague, content is redundant, and information is hard to find
  • Mundane color scheme

Solutions

  • Update with responsive design
  • Reduce text and increase icon and image use
  • Simplify navigation categories with meaningful labels
  • Update color, logo, and font for aesthetic appeal

User Survey

When writing the user survey, I found that I had to adjust my research objectives. Rather than trying to find out how users find resources for disability services, I changed my objective to finding out how users would go about volunteering with a non-profit. Survey was disseminated via Facebook and came back with 19 responses.

╲╱

Majority of responders found non-profits to be poorly designed with important information being too hard to find.

User Interviews

The interview questions focused on why and how people volunteer and what they want from an organization’s website.

╲╱

Interviewees shared that they wanted to see pictures of people, to see who and what they are and do. They wanted to know how they could help and what would be expected of them as volunteers.

User Persona

As we refined the user persona, we also refined the scope of this project by making the main user task signing up to volunteer with disABILITYsa. On the current site, it is a confusing process with several different forms that mostly ask the same questions, and each field is required. We simplified the process by making one volunteer page where a user like Tanya could go straight to it from the home page, fill out one simple form, and receive all the information she needs.

Information Architecture

The current site navigation is overwhelming because of the vaguely labeled categories and repetition of page listings. Restructuring the navigation was a key part of this site’s redesign, not only because it’s good practice but also because clear navigation is an essential feature in an accessible site. I led my team in a card-sorting activity and created the subsequent site map. Information architecture is a particular interest of mine, and I am especially skilled at restructuring websites.

Wireframes

The main driving factor throughout the process was accessibility. We wanted our design to be clean, simple, and easy to navigate; therefore, we used blue as our primary color and green as the single accent color to highlight donations. Every new feature was tested against web accessibility standards along the way.

Starting with the wireframes, I incorporated iconography to add to the accessibility of the page. During user testing, many commented on how helpful the visual menu was because a lot of people process images better than text. The layout was purposefully “standard.” We felt that meeting expectations was more important than flashy design. We did, however, stray from the standard when we went with the tiled menu rather than the typical mobile dropdown.

Updating the Look

Part of the redesign included the logo and color scheme. We didn’t want to change the brand, just update it. We recreated the logo using a new font and used a more saturated blue as the primary color.

Testers also made useful suggestions for improvement:

  • Make the menu icons white on the desktop - I had originally made them black becuase I was concerned about the menu getting too cluttered, but users all agreed that the white icons were better

Summary

Goals Achieved:
✔ Meaningful reorganization of information and categories
✔ Livelier color scheme & clean, simple design
✔ More accessible & user-centered
✔ Simplified volunteer sign-up

Obstacles:
The biggest obstacle we faced was defining the scope of the project. disABILITYsa is a great organization that should have a great site, so we wanted to do as much as possible for them. However, because we only had three weeks to complete the project, we had to choose one to two objectives. Additionally, in order to prioritize our objectives, we had to choose the right user. Who visits this site? The obvious answer is someone looking for disability services, but the plethora and spectrum of disability made it challenging to define a user and finding relevant users to interview (especially during a pandemic). Because disABILITYsa is an organization that offers volunteer opportunities, we decided that would be the best route to take. By defining the user as someone (disabled or not) who wanted to volunteer with the organization, we were able to find relevant interviewees and narrow the scope of the project to home page, navigation, and volunteer sign-up.
